Stage Management Experience

I discovered my passion for stage management when I was 12 years old, as I got the opportunity to be the assistant stage manager for a high school production of Rent. I was fascinated by the backstage world, where I could see, hear, and feel the magic of theater. I learned how to coordinate the actors, the crew, and the technical elements, and how to handle the pressure and excitement of live performance. Below is a list of some of the most memorable shows I worked on, and the challenges and achievements I experienced in each role.

WillyWonka: I was the stage manager for the production of Charlie and the Chocolate Factory, a musical adaptation of the classic novel by Roald Dahl. As the stage manager, I was responsible for coordinating all the technical and artistic aspects of the show, from the pre-production stage to the final performance.

Some of my main tasks and achievements as the stage manager were:

  • I participated in all the production meetings from mid-September 2018, where I collaborated with the producer, director, set designer, costume designer, lighting designer, sound designer, and musical director. I was the only student in these meetings, and I contributed with my ideas and feedback to the creative process.
  • I assisted in the auditions and casting of the actors, and I attended multiple rehearsals per week from October to December 2018. I checked and reported the attendance of all the actors, and I monitored their progress and performance. I also gave them notes and instructions on their cues, blocking, and movements.
  • I selected and trained the backstage crew, which consisted of eight students who helped with the set changes, props, costumes, and special effects. I supervised their work and ensured that they followed the safety protocols and quality standards.
  • I called the show, which means that I communicated with the actors, crew, and orchestra through headsets and speakers during the performances. I gave them the signals to start and end each scene, song, and act. I also managed the transitions between the scenes, and I dealt with any unexpected issues or emergencies that occurred on stage or backstage.
  • I was in charge of the storage and maintenance of all the equipment, sets, props, and costumes after each show. I made sure that everything was properly organized, cleaned, and repaired for the next show. I also kept an inventory of all the materials and reported any damages or losses.
  • I adapted to the challenges of working with a remote set designer, who was based in Europe and only arrived in New York eight days before the show. We had to coordinate the design and construction of the sets through Skype calls and emails, and we had to make some last-minute changes and adjustments to fit the space and budget.

In this role, I learned and developed many skills and qualities, such as:

  1. Leadership: I led a team of more than 30 people, including actors, crew, and orchestra. I motivated them, supported them, and resolved any conflicts or problems that arose among them.
  2. Management: I organized and planned all the logistics and resources of the show, such as the schedule, budget, equipment, and materials. I also kept track of the tasks and goals of each department and ensured that they were completed on time and within the scope and quality expected.
  3. Improvisation: I was able to think quickly and creatively when faced with unforeseen situations or difficulties, such as when some student actors got locked in the dressing room, the door handle broke, and I suggested they called maintenance to break in, and then we duct-taped the bolt so this wouldn't happen again.
  4. Troubleshooting: I was able to identify and fix any errors or malfunctions that occurred with the technology, equipment, or sets during the rehearsals or the performances. I also prevented any potential risks or hazards by following the safety measures and contingency plans.
  5. Communication: I communicated effectively and professionally with a diverse and multidisciplinary team of people, both in person and online. I used clear and concise language, and I adapted my tone and style to the audience and the purpose of the message.
  6. Collaboration: I worked cooperatively and harmoniously with the director, the producer, and the other designers and artists involved in the show. I respected their vision and opinions, and I integrated their feedback and suggestions into my work.

My Experience as a Stage Manager and Prop Master

I have been involved in several theater productions, both as a stage manager and a prop master. In these roles, I learned how to coordinate, organize, and create the technical and artistic elements of a show. Here are some of the highlights of my work:

  • Twelfth Night: I was the stage manager for this Shakespearean comedy, which had a cast of about 25 actors and a crew of 5 technicians. Sadly, due to covid, we were forced to make our show digital, having each member of the cast film their own videos and the director edited them together. This was a challenging but rewarding experience, as I had to communicate with the actors and the crew online, and ensure that they followed the script, the cues, and the deadlines. I also had to troubleshoot any technical issues that arose during the filming or the editing process.
  • Grease The Musical: I was the prop master for this popular musical, which featured a lot of props, such as costumes, accessories, and a full-size car that was rolled on to the stage. Our set designer lived abroad and came over for only one week to build the set, so I had to work closely with him and the director to make sure that the props matched the design and the vision of the show. I also had to supervise the prop crew, who helped me with the storage, maintenance, and transportation of the props.
  • Xanadu: I was a member of the backstage crew for this fantasy musical, which had a lot of special effects, such as lighting, sound, and roller skates. There were 7 other people in the crew, and I and one other member of the crew were in charge of everything on stage left. We had to move the sets, the props, and the wires, and make sure that they were in the right place and in the right condition. We also had to assist the actors with their quick changes and their entrances and exits.
  • Romeo & Juliet: I was the prop master for this classic tragedy, which required a lot of props, such as swords, daggers, potions, and flowers. This involved going through all the props in the school drama department storage and helping select, identify and place orders to purchase what was missing. I also had to organize all the props to ensure they were ready to go on stage, and label them with the names of the characters and the scenes. At the end of each show, the props needed to be checked and sorted again to be ready for the next show.

These are some of the shows that I am most proud of, and that helped me develop my skills and passion for theater. I hope you enjoyed reading about them.

The Cult that Made Spoons

In the summer of 2023, I had the amazing opportunity to participate in the Edinburgh Fringe Festival, the world's largest arts festival, with a UNIS theater group. We wrote and produced an original play called "The Cult that Made Spoons: A Fork in the Road", a comedy about a group of spoon enthusiasts who get involved in a conspiracy. We performed one show in New York, where we received positive feedback and encouragement from the audience and the critics. Then we flew to Edinburgh, where we performed four shows for the public in a small venue. We had to compete with hundreds of other shows, but we managed to attract a decent crowd and make them laugh with our witty dialogue and hilarious situations. We also got to watch some of the other shows and learn from the diversity and creativity of the performers. It was an unforgettable experience that enriched my artistic and cultural horizons.
To help fund the cost of this trip, which was around $3000 per person, I sold popcorn during the parent-teacher conferences and after school. I made the popcorn myself, using different flavors and toppings, such as caramel, cheese, and chocolate. I also decorated the popcorn bags with catchy slogans and pictures related to our play. I sold each bag for $2, and I managed to raise about $600, which was 20% of the cost of my trip. I was proud of my initiative and entrepreneurship, and I was grateful for the support of my teachers, classmates, and parents.
